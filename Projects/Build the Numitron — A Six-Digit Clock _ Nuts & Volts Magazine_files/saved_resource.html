                              Bill van Dijk john_jackson@cox.net 8 months ago

                              Hi John,

                              When the clock is starting, and all the LEDS are flashing, press and hold the MINUTE button, and than the HOUR button. Hold both buttons, and the display will alternate between 12 and 24. Let go of the buttons when the desired mode is lit.
                              Again, only at start up, when you press and hold the HOUR button, and then the MINUTE button, the display will count 00, 01, to 08 and back to 00. This is the LED pattern selection. Most people like the 00, this is where the clock changes pattern every hour.

                              Now that we know the wires to the tube are correct, the connections 9 to 15 between the 4511 (the chip under the tube) and the tube itself must also be correct. The same (BCD coded) data for the tubes arrives to ALL the 4511 chips at the same time, but only the chip selected by the PIC (via the 74HC164 shift register) will latch the data in. Since the other tubes are working correctly, the data on the BCD buss is good, and the 74HC164 is working. This leads me to suspect a bad or cold solder joint on one of the pins 1, to 7 on the 4511 under the affected tube.

                              Unfortunately this one is not easy to get to, but please resolder these connections, and try again.

                              Bill

                                                Hi Dave,
                                                The accuracy of the clock is determined by the 32.762kHz crystal and its load capacitors. In the article I wrote about it, and at this point I can only suggest to replace them with known good parts. The LED flicker is most likely due to a bad LED. have a close look, and find the pattern.                                                       Hi Chris,
                                                      There are a number of compromises made in the design. The LEDs I recommend are the ultra bright type so that once they are multiplexed ( 1:8 in this design) will have enough brightness left. In fact, even with this in mind I used a rather high resistance in the LED circuit to end up with what I believed to be a reasonable brightness. It is not designed to light the room or be blinding. That said, there are a lot of different LEDs on the market, as well as a lot of opinions about what is pleasing. Anyway, if you want the LEDs a bit brighter, you could lower the value of the resistors R1 to R8. Offhand what you describe does not sound like a problem to me. I could be wrong; I can't see what you do.
                                                      The missing LEDs is a different matter. 4, 12, 20, and 28 are on the same column, 33, 41, 49, and 57 are also grouped together, but on a different column. If this is the only pattern that has an anomaly, than that is really strange. My first suspicion would be an issue with the reset line on the shift register pin 15 on the 4017. But that should also affect other patterns. I would suggest taking a very close look at all connections on the PIC and the 4017. Also, take a very close look at individual LEDs. If one seems a bit different than the others, try replacing it. Bad or "leaky" LEDs can cause weird problems. Good luck, and don't hesitate to write again if you have further observations.

                                                                              Bill van Dijk Points 4 years ago

                                                                              I'm glad you got it fixed. The reason that one LED can cause this sort of problem lies in their failure modes and the multiplexing scheme used in the clock. Despite appearances, there are never more than 8 LEDs lit at any given time. They switch fast, and therefore our eyes perceive them as all lit at the same time. The PIC sends a signal to an entire column of LEDs, but the ULN2803 grounds only one row so any individual LED can be addressed. When one LED becomes leaky, it allows adjacent rows to ground through the leaky LED, and partially light up. Now you can see where the odd behavior comes from.
                                                                              Send me a picture of your case when done, I'd love to see it!

The fuse is connected directly to the power socket, so if the power supply puts out 12.1 Volt, you should have 12.1 (or close to that) at the fuse.                                                                                                                   Bill van Dijk Richard Lee 4 years ago

                                                                                                                  That's great. BTW, those were real nice little scopes. I still have a 10-4550, and although not much use to me anymore, I just can't bring myself to get rid of it! So, yes, warm it up and we will use it.
                                                                                                                  set the scope for DC mode, 1V-cm, trigger on Y1+ and timebase 0.1ms. The ground of the probe must be connected to the VSS of the clock, you can use the center pin of the ICSP connector, or the metal bracket of a button. Now plug in the clock, and leave it on its flash startup cycle. Your numitrons should be flashing all 8's.
                                                                                                                  On the 4017 chip: (adjust your timebase to give a nice parade of pulses)
                                                                                                                  look for clock pulses on PIN14. On pin 15 you should see reset pulses at a lower (1/7th) frequency. On its output side you should see nice regular pulses on pins 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 9, and 10.
                                                                                                                  On the ULN2803 these pulses enter on pins 1 to 8. They exit on pins 11 to 18. Pin 9 must be ground.

                                                                                                                  Since the numitrons are working, I fully expect the pulses entering the 4017 to be there and correct. If not, there could be a short or break in the circuit board between the 4017 and the PIC.
                                                                                                                  If there are pulses exiting the 4017, than it is working.
                                                                                                                  At the ULN2803 I expect the pulses in to be OK if the 4017 is working, if not, there is a break in the board or the resistors R21 to 28. Not likely though. If there are no pulses on the output side the ULN2803 may be defective, or something is shorted. If the pulses are there, the LEDs are somehow defective.

                                                                                                                  Anyhow, go give this a go, and let me know what you find.

                                                                                                                  Bill

                                                                                                                                Bill van Dijk Gary 5 years ago

                                                                                                                                Hi Gary, sorry for the tardy response; I was out of town for a while. The next thing is to look for is if the two crystals are running at the correct speed. Do you have a scope or frequency counter handy? The main program must run at (about or close to) 4 MHz, and the small watch crystal as close as possible to 32.768KHz. A mismatch between the watch crystal and load capacitors is often the reason it runs at the wrong frequency, if at all. If unsure, replace the watch crystal with a known part with a load capacity of 6.5pF (this is what the clock was designed for).

                                                                                                                                Bill
